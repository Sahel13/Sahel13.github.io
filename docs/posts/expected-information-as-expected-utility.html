Expected Information as Expected Utility

In Bayesian experimental design, the expected information gain (Lindley 1956)

EIG(ξ)≔Ep(y∣ξ)[H[pθ(θ)]−H[pθ∣y,ξ(θ)]],\mathrm{EIG}(\xi) \coloneqq \mathbb{E}_{p(y \mid \xi)} \Bigl[ \mathbb{H} \bigl[ p_{\theta}(\theta) \bigr] - \mathbb{H} \bigl[ p_{\theta \mid y, \xi}(\theta) \bigr] \Bigr],EIG(ξ):=Ep(y∣ξ)​[H[pθ​(θ)]−H[pθ∣y,ξ​(θ)]],

has become the predominant metric for evaluating the quality of an experimental design ξ\xiξ. It measures how much information we expect to gain about the unknown parameters θ∈Θ\theta \in \Thetaθ∈Θ after observing data yyy generated under that design. Here, H\mathbb{H}H denotes Shannon entropy (or differential entropy, for continuous θ\thetaθ), and the expectation is taken over all possible observations under the design.

The EIG is intuitive—it is the expected reduction in entropy from prior to posterior. But intuition aside, it raises a natural question. The reduction in Shannon entropy is only one of many ways to measure uncertainty reduction, or even more generally, the utility of an experiment (ξ,y)(\xi, y)(ξ,y) (Huan et al. 2024). Is there, then, a fundamental reason to prefer the EIG over alternative utility measures?

It turns out the answer is yes. In Bernardo (1979), José M. Bernardo, a student of Dennis Lindley, gave a justification grounded in decision theory. He showed that the EIG arises naturally from a decision problem under reasonable assumptions on the utility function. I find this result extremely remarkable, and this is an appreciation post about half-a-century later :)

The Decision-Theoretic Setup

Consider a decision problem where a scientist has to report a distribution q∈P(Θ)q \in \mathcal{P}(\Theta)q∈P(Θ) for θ\thetaθ, where the decision space P(Θ)\mathcal{P}(\Theta)P(Θ) is the set of probability measures on Θ\ThetaΘ.1 The utility obtained if she reports qqq when the true parameter is θ\thetaθ is quantified by means of a utility function u:P(Θ)×Θ→Ru:\mathcal{P}(\Theta) \times \Theta \to \mathbb{R}u:P(Θ)×Θ→R.2 Suppose the scientist applies a design ξ\xiξ and observes an outcome yyy, after which she updates her belief to pθ∣y,ξ(θ)p_{\theta \mid y, \xi}(\theta)pθ∣y,ξ​(θ) using Bayes’ rule. Then, her expected utility when reporting a distribution qqq is

∫u(q,θ) pθ∣y,ξ(dθ).\int u(q, \theta) \, p_{\theta \mid y, \xi}(d\theta).∫u(q,θ)pθ∣y,ξ​(dθ).

Under the principle of maximum expected utility, the scientist should report the distribution q∗q^{*}q∗ that maximizes the above integral, which may not be her actual belief pθ∣y,ξp_{\theta \mid y, \xi}pθ∣y,ξ​. Hence, to discourage lying, we require that uuu is a strictly proper utility function, meaning that

sup⁡q∫u(q,θ) pθ∣y,ξ(dθ)=∫u(pθ∣y,ξ,θ) pθ∣y,ξ(dθ)\sup_{q} \int u(q, \theta) \, p_{\theta \mid y, \xi}(d\theta) = \int u(p_{\theta \mid y, \xi}, \theta) \, p_{\theta \mid y, \xi}(d\theta)qsup​∫u(q,θ)pθ∣y,ξ​(dθ)=∫u(pθ∣y,ξ​,θ)pθ∣y,ξ​(dθ)

with the supremum only attained at q∗=pθ∣y,ξq^{*} = p_{\theta \mid y, \xi}q∗=pθ∣y,ξ​. In other words, truth-telling should be the optimal strategy.

The second restriction we need on the utility function is that it is local, i.e., u(q,θ)=u(q(θ),θ)u(q, \theta) = u\bigl(q(\theta), \theta\bigr)u(q,θ)=u(q(θ),θ) for all θ∈Θ\theta \in \Thetaθ∈Θ. Locality means the utility function only depends on the density of the reported distribution at the true parameter θ\thetaθ, which feels reasonable (though admittedly not as easy to motivate as properness). The striking result of Bernardo (1979) is that if the utility function uuu is proper, local, and sufficiently smooth (as a function of qqq), then it must be of the form

u(q,θ)=Alog⁡q(θ)+B(θ),u(q, \theta) = A \log q(\theta) + B(\theta),u(q,θ)=Alogq(θ)+B(θ),

where the constant AAA and function BBB can be arbitrary. Plugging this back into the requirement that uuu is proper, we see that the maximum expected utility is

sup⁡q∫u(q,θ) pθ∣y,ξ(dθ)=∫(Alog⁡pθ∣y,ξ(θ)+B(θ))pθ∣y,ξ(dθ)=−AH[p(θ∣x,ξ)]+Eθ∣y,ξ[B(θ)].\begin{align*} \sup_{q} \int u(q, \theta) \, p_{\theta \mid y, \xi}(d\theta) & = \int \left( A \log p_{\theta \mid y, \xi}(\theta) + B(\theta) \right) p_{\theta \mid y, \xi}(d\theta) \\ & = -A \mathbb{H}\left[ p(\theta \mid x, \xi) \right] + \mathbb{E}_{\theta \mid y, \xi}[B(\theta)]. \end{align*}qsup​∫u(q,θ)pθ∣y,ξ​(dθ)​=∫(Alogpθ∣y,ξ​(θ)+B(θ))pθ∣y,ξ​(dθ)=−AH[p(θ∣x,ξ)]+Eθ∣y,ξ​[B(θ)].​

Lo and behold, the Shannon entropy appears! How cool is that?

Back to Experimental Design

Now, the gain in expected utility for the scientist from the experiment (ξ,y)(\xi, y)(ξ,y) is

which simplifies to

Finally, to assess the quality of a design ξ\xiξ, we take the expectation over the observation distribution p(y∣ξ)p(y \mid \xi)p(y∣ξ), leaving us with

This shows that, for the purpose of choosing the best design, the choice of AAA and BBB is irrelevant, and the utility function can simply be the log density, u(q,θ)=log⁡q(θ)u(q, \theta) = \log q(\theta)u(q,θ)=logq(θ).

Concluding Remarks

To motivate his paper, Bernardo writes in the abstract:

… a scientist typically does not have, nor can be normally expected to have, a clear idea of the utility of his results.

By considering a decision problem of choosing the best distribution to report, and showing that the EIG arises naturally under reasonable assumptions on the utility function, Bernardo makes a compelling argument for using the EIG in precisely those settings where the scientist cannot quantify the utility of her results apriori.

Note that the whole argument breaks down if the prior pθp_{\theta}pθ​ or the likelihood p(y∣θ,ξ)p(y \mid \theta, \xi)p(y∣θ,ξ) is misspecified, because then the posterior pθ∣y,ξp_{\theta \mid y, \xi}pθ∣y,ξ​ given by Bayes’ rule is not valid.
